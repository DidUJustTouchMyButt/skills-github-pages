The Story

In Bremerton, a town painted in Navy blue and shades of grey, guitarist Bub Pratt and bassist Andrew share a house and a scholarship to Olympic College. Bub, a poet who dances outside the lines, and Andrew, meticulous and driven, become unlikely anchors for a burgeoning creative scene.

Amidst jam sessions fueled by Pizneon Greens and bootleg coffee, open mics at the divey Old Town Tavern, and the constant rhythm of Bub's vintage Remington typewriter – the *tingg* Machine – a community sparks. They navigate disastrous gigs, complicated relationships (divorces, unexpected pregnancies, the magnetic pull of new connections like Mary and Karen), black market dealings orchestrated by the enigmatic Bill Lawson, and the raw, messy process of finding their artistic voices against a blue-collar backdrop.

It's a story about jazz, poetry, resilience, and the accidental renaissance ignited by cheap beer, ambition, and the refusal to let the landscape remain colorless.

The World: Rat City Rhapsody

The air hangs thick with the smell of brine and rain. This isn't Seattle's polish; it's Bremerton, Silverdale, Kingston – working towns huddled by the Puget Sound. Life revolves around the Navy base, the ferry schedules, and the local dives like the Old Town Tavern (home of Pizneon Greens and Fred's crooner past) or Bill's Grotto (part speakeasy, part black market hub).

The Olympic College music department is a haven, its practice rooms echoing with scales and standards, a stark contrast to the "drab Navy town" outside. The Scott Street house becomes ground zero for late-night jams and chaotic camaraderie.

The *tingg* Machine

Central to Bub's world is his late 40's Remington portable typewriter, the tingg* Machine. It's not just a tool for his zines; it's a percussion instrument, a character, the relentless rhythm section for his spoken jazz dreams. Its *tingg* punctuates the narrative, a mechanical heartbeat in the digital age.

Other key spots bubble up: The Bebop Coffeeshop (a source of inspiration and bad jobs), the Manette Bridge (a landmark for contemplation and dares), the Kingston Marina (a bohemian escape on a leaky boat), and the ubiquitous VW Wonderbus (Bub's home, transport, and mobile creative space).

The Sound: Notes & Words

The soundtrack is pure Bebop – complex, fast, improvisational. Tunes like "Donna Lee," "So What?," "Mr. P.C.," "Nardis," and "Confirmation" aren't just background music; they're the language the characters speak, the challenges they tackle in jam sessions.

Overlaying this is the Narration, delivered like beat/slam poetry – rhythmic, raw, weaving through the story with the persistent *tingg* of Bub's typewriter. Bub's own Spoken Jazz performances blend his poetry directly with the music, blurring the lines between words and notes.

Original songs pepper the narrative, reflecting character moments: Sydney's electronic "Lemons," Karen's heartbroken "Stupid Gullible Me," the hopeful "Butterfly Song," the raw anger of "Fires & Floods," and the poignant "Wedding Waltz."

Bub's zines ("Bebop Coffeeshop," etc.) represent the DIY, tangible aspect of his creativity, crafted with scissors, glue, and the *tingg* Machine.

The Poet: Bub Pratt

Bub Pratt channels the spirit of the beat poets and the improvisational energy of jazz into narratives that explore the messy beauty of creative life in unexpected places. Drawing from experiences in the Pacific Northwest music scene, Pratt's work captures the rhythm of late nights, the sting of cheap whiskey, and the relentless drive to make art against the odds.
